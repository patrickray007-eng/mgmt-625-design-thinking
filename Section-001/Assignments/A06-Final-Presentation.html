Tell the Story of Your Project

Overview

Present the complete story of your design thinking project in a 9-minute block. Format is your choice (film, live demo, installation, performance, or other). Tell a compelling, evidence-grounded story and involve the audience as participants, not just spectators.

Learning Objectives

  • Tell a complete problem-to-solution narrative grounded in research evidence
  • Demonstrate full traceability from empathy work to prototype
  • Build and test a prototype with real stakeholders
  • Show iteration and refinement based on feedback and stress testing
  • Choose a communication format intentionally, matching medium to message

What to Do

  1. Tell a three-act story:
  • Act 1 (The World As It Is): Introduce stakeholders and the problem through specific observations and real quotes. Make the audience feel the problem.
  • Act 2 (What You Learned): Show how research, causal loop analysis, and stress testing deepened your understanding. What changed from mid-project to now?
  • Act 3 (What You Built): Show the prototype you put in front of real people. What happened, what worked, what didn't, what would you test next?
  1. Build and test a prototype with at least one real stakeholder before presenting. It can be rough (paper, cardboard, digital mockup, role-played service), but it must have been used by a real person.
  2. Involve the audience meaningfully during your 9 minutes (discussion prompt, interactive moment, artifact, live demo).
  3. Submit a format rationale (one paragraph) 48 hours before Session 12: what format, why it serves your story, any special setup needs beyond projector and screen.

Submit

Format rationale to Canvas 48 hours before Session 12. Present in class Session 12 (Monday, March 2), 9-minute block. Upload supporting materials to Canvas after session.

Specifications

1. Problem Clarity Met: Audience understands who experiences the problem, what it is, and why it matters, with specific stakeholders and contexts.

2. Research Evidence Met: Story includes 3+ concrete examples from research (specific quotes, observations, or moments from empathy work).

3. Traceability Met: Clear narrative thread from research to insights to problem to solution. Audience can follow how discoveries led to the prototype direction.

4. Prototype and Testing Met: Team built a prototype and tested it with at least one real stakeholder. Testing results (including what didn't work) are shared.

5. Iteration Evidence Met: Story shows how thinking evolved from mid-project to final, with specific changes based on gallery walk feedback and stress testing.

6. Format Intentionality and Audience Engagement Met: Format choice is justified in the written rationale, medium serves the story, and audience is involved meaningfully during the 9-minute block.
