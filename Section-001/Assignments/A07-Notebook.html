Design Thinking Notebook

Overview

Submit your notebook containing the raw material of your design thinking practice: observations, sketches, maps, reflections, and in-class activities. The notebook values process over presentation and sustained engagement over perfect documentation.

Learning Objectives

  • Maintain a continuous practice of documentation throughout the design process
  • Capture observations, ideas, and reflections in real-time
  • Create a personal reference for design thinking practices
  • Develop the habit of externalizing thinking

What to Do

Your notebook should include these core entries:

  1. TETHER Observation: Full 90-minute observation notes, assumptions in margins, and reflection.
  2. Find the Duct Tape (weekly, Sessions 1-11): Each week, document one "duct tape" solution you encounter (a workaround or improvised fix). Record what it is, what problem it solves, and why the real fix doesn't exist.
  3. Stakeholder map drafts from your team project.
  4. Causal loop drafts and iterations of your causal loop map.
  5. Problem definition iterations showing how your thinking evolved.

In-class notes are optional but helpful for demonstrating engagement (activity notes, ideation outputs, feedback received, reflections).

Submit

Physical notebooks: collected in class Session 12 (Monday, March 2), returned after grading. Digital notebooks: submitted on Canvas before class.

Specifications

1. TETHER Documentation Met: Substantial TETHER observation notes (3-4+ pages) reflecting 90 minutes of sustained observation, with assumptions noted and reflection present.

2. Session Coverage Met: Entries from each major course phase: Sessions 1-4 (individual observation), Sessions 5-8 (team problem definition), Sessions 9-11 (ideation and testing).

3. Sustained Engagement Met: Entries show real-time documentation (context-specific details, natural style variation across sessions, not reconstructible in one sitting).

4. Thinking Made Visible Met: Shows actual thinking through drafts, iterations, questions, and dead ends. Includes visual thinking (stakeholder maps, causal loop attempts, ideation sketches).

5. Legibility Met: Notebook is readable, entries are roughly chronological, and it's clear which entries correspond to which sessions/activities.
