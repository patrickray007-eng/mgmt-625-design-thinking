Causal Loop Map

Overview

Create a causal loop map that visualizes the system dynamics underlying your team's problem, showing why it persists: the feedback loops, reinforcing cycles, and structural factors that keep it in place. Submit with a half-page written explanation of the key dynamics.

Learning Objectives

  • Analyze problems as systems with feedback loops and interdependencies
  • Identify reinforcing and balancing loops that perpetuate problems
  • Recognize second-order effects and unintended consequences
  • Understand who benefits from problem persistence (and why solutions may face resistance)

What to Do

  1. Start with your core problem at the center. Be specific (not "communication issues" but "frontline staff feedback doesn't reach decision-makers").
  2. Identify contributing factors and draw arrows pointing toward the problem.
  3. Trace the effects and draw arrows pointing away from the problem.
  4. Find the loops. Where do effects become causes? Label with R (reinforcing) or B (balancing). You need at least 2 feedback loops.
  5. Mark relationships with +/- signs: (+) = as A increases, B increases; (-) = as A increases, B decreases.
  6. Identify who benefits from the current state, even unintentionally, and why they might resist change.
  7. Write a half-page explanation of the most important dynamics and how they connect to your stakeholder research.

Your map should include at least 8-10 nodes, at least 2 feedback loops, and annotations explaining at least 2 loops plus 1 "who benefits" insight. Create as a digital diagram or legible hand-drawn scan.

Draft your causal loop map in your notebook first. This draft work is assessed as part of your notebook grade.

Submit

Causal loop map (image or PDF) and half-page written explanation to Canvas before Session 4 (Thursday, March 26).

Specifications

1. System Complexity Met: 8-10+ distinct nodes specific to the team's scenario, with plausible relationships grounded in research or observation.

2. Feedback Loop Identification Met: At least 2 feedback loops identified, labeled (R or B), and explained in the written section with clear logic for why they reinforce or balance.

3. Relationship Clarity Met: Arrows clearly show direction of influence with +/- signs that make logical sense. A reader can trace the system dynamics by following the arrows.

4. "Who Benefits" Analysis Met: Identifies at least one stakeholder who benefits from the problem persisting (even unintentionally) and explains why this creates resistance to change.

5. Explanation Quality Met: Half-page explanation illuminates the most important dynamics, explains why the loops matter, and connects the map to the team's stakeholder research.
