Is Your Problem Statement Narrow Enough?
A good problem statement passes all five:
1. Can you name the specific people affected?
Not "employees" but "new nurses in their first rotation at a
community hospital."
2. Can you point to where it happens?
A specific place, process, or moment. Not a general condition.
3. Could you observe it in a single visit?
If you'd need a month of data to even see the problem, it's
too broad.
4. Does your research support it?
Can you trace the statement back to something a stakeholder
said or you directly observed?
5. Could a team of five test a response in 5 days?
If the problem requires policy change or organizational
restructuring to address, narrow further.
TOO BROAD
"New employees struggle to onboard effectively."
GETTING CLOSER
"New nurses don't know who to ask for help during their first
rotation."
NARROW ENOUGH
"New nurses at community hospitals rely on informal hallway
conversations to learn which unwritten protocols vary by unit,
because no formal handoff exists between orientation and
floor work."

Frame Your Hypothesis
Pick your 1-2 strongest concepts from the grid. Reframe each as a testable hypothesis:
"We believe [solution] will [outcome] for [stakeholder] because [insight from our research]."
The "because" clause is the test. It must trace to something a stakeholder actually said or did during your research. If it doesn't point to
evidence, you're guessing.
Write your hypotheses on large paper and post them on the wall. Then walk the room and read every team's hypothesis silently. No presenting.
 ~30 minutes (framing + posting + reading)

Assumption Audit
You just read each other's hypotheses. Now find the weakest link in your own.
Team Activity
1. What makes you nervous about your own hypothesis?
2. List 5 critical assumptions it depends on
3. Rank by lethality: "If this is wrong, our concept fails"
4. For your top 2: what specific, observable evidence would disprove this?
 ~15 minutes
 Confidence without evidence is just optimism. If two people can't agree on whether your kill criterion has been met,
it's not specific enough.

Adversarial Stress Test
Find weaknesses, not strengths.
1. Half your team stays at your table as defenders. Other half walks to another team's table as attackers.
2. Attackers: find the assumption that, if wrong, wastes their five days.
3. 12 minutes. Then swap roles at a different table.
 ~25 minutes (12 min each round)
Questions worth asking:
"You're assuming the stakeholder wants this. Do you know that, or do you believe that?"
"What evidence do you have beyond your own intuition?"
"What if your problem definition is wrong?"
"Who benefits if this works, and who doesn't?"

Three Tools, One Company
Before Thursday, you need to run an experiment. Here's what that means, and what it doesn't.
MVP
Built to Run
A product you ship and keep
running. It's rough, but it works as a
service. You grow it into a business.
SURVIVAL TACTIC
Built to Not Die
Pure scrappiness. Whatever it takes
to keep the lights on and prove you
won't quit.
PROTOTYPE
Built to Learn
Tests one assumption with one real
person. Might be disposable. Not
built to last. Built to find out if
you're right.
You're building a prototype. Not an MVP. Not a business. A test.

The MVP: Air Mattresses, 2007
MVP
Chesky and Gebbia can't make rent. Design conference in SF, hotels
sold out. They buy three air mattresses, build a simple website: AirBed
and Breakfast. $80/night. Breakfast included.
Three strangers show up and pay.
They kept running it. Kept hosting.
It became the business.

The Survival Tactic: Cereal Boxes, 2008
SURVIVAL
One year later. Airbnb is nearly dead. No funding, no traction, massive
debt. The 2008 election is happening.
They design custom cereal: Obama O's and Cap'n McCains. 500 boxes
each. $20 to make, $40 to sell. They sell out. $30,000. Enough to keep
the lights on.
Paul Graham, Y Combinator:
"If you can convince people to pay $40 for a $4 box of cereal, you can
convince people to stay in each other's homes. You are cockroaches.
You will not die."

The Prototype: Photography, 2009
PROTOTYPE
Airbnb has users, but nobody's booking. Hypothesis: the photos are
terrible and that's killing trust.
They rent a camera, fly to New York, knock on hosts' doors. One week.
One apartment at a time.
2-3x more bookings.
Revenue doubled that month.
Then they threw away the prototype
and built the real program.
"A web startup would say, 'Let's send emails, teach users
professional photography, and test them.' We said, 'Screw that.'"
"When we fixed the product in New York, it solved our problems
in Paris, London, Vancouver, and Miami."
Chesky & Gebbia, Fast Company

Design Your Experiment
Team Activity: Answer four questions. Write them on your large paper.
1. What's the riskiest assumption you're testing?
2. What's the smallest thing you can do to test it?
Not the whole solution. Just the test.
3. What does "pass" look like? What does "fail" look like?
Specific enough that two people can agree on whether it happened.
4. Who specifically will you test with, and when?
Name a real person. Pick a day.
 ~45 minutes
Plus: task assignments. Each team member's name, their specific action, their stakeholder, the day they'll do it.
