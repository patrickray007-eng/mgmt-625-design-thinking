# Problem Reflection

## Overview

Write a 400-600 word reflection that closes Project 1 (individual observation work) and prepares you for Project 2 (team-based scenario work). You'll examine what you learned about observation, problem identification, and your own tendencies as a designer.

## Learning Objectives

- Synthesize learning from [[A01-TETHER-Observation|TETHER observation]] and Session 2 activities
- Articulate the difference between observing and interpreting
- Identify personal patterns and growth areas in design thinking practice
- Connect individual observation experience to upcoming team work

## What to Do

1. **Reflect on the observation experience.** What did you learn from your [[A01-TETHER-Observation|TETHER]] experience? What was harder or easier than expected? How did hearing about others' observations (from different environments) change your perspective?
2. **Examine problems and assumptions.** What problem or friction did you observe? What assumptions did you make about why it exists? Which were actually supported by observation vs. inferred?
3. **Look forward to Project 2.** What will you carry from Project 1 into your team's work? What would you do differently when observing stakeholders?

## Submit

PDF or Word document to Canvas before Session 3 (Thursday, March 12). 400-600 words addressing all three parts.

## Specifications

**1. Substantive Engagement with Observation Experience**
Met: Includes specific examples from TETHER and Session 2 activities, demonstrates genuine reflection on learning (not just description of activities), addresses challenges and insights with specificity.

**2. Distinction Between Observation and Interpretation**
Met: Clearly articulates the difference between observable facts and inferences. Provides specific examples of assumptions made and examines whether they were supported by evidence.

**3. Forward Application**
Met: Identifies specific, actionable insights to carry into Project 2. Connects individual observation learning to upcoming team-based stakeholder research.

**4. Length and Completeness**
Met: 400-600 words. All three parts addressed. Writing is clear and organized.
