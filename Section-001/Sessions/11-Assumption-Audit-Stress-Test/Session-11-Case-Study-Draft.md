## The Concept

Every team has spent 10 sessions building a case from primary research. Today we treat it like one. The session runs like a case discussion, except the authors are in the room and the class gets to interrogate their analysis directly.

This framing does three things:
1. Speaks the language students already respect (case method)
2. Elevates what they've done: you didn't read a case, you wrote one
3. Makes the stress test feel like a natural MBA activity, not a design exercise

---

## Session Flow

### 2:15-2:25 | Opening: Frame the Case (10 min)

> "In every other MBA course, you walk into class having read a case someone else wrote about a company you've never visited, based on interviews you didn't conduct. Today is different. You're presenting a case you built from scratch."

> "Over ten sessions, you did the fieldwork. You interviewed stakeholders. You modeled the system with causal loops. You defined the problem and generated solution hypotheses. That's a complete case. The difference between yours and a Harvard case is that the authors are in the room, and the class gets to interrogate the analysis directly."

> "Here's how today works. Three phases, same structure as any case discussion."

Put on screen:

| Phase | What You Do | Time |
|-------|------------|------|
| **Case Prep** | Your team identifies the 5 critical assumptions in your analysis. Which ones would change your entire recommendation if they turned out to be wrong? | 15 min |
| **Decision Criteria** | For your top 2 assumptions, define the evidence that would kill your recommendation. Not "if people don't like it." Specific, observable, agreed-upon thresholds. | 15 min |
| **Case Discussion** | Teams pair up. One team presents their case. The other team's job: find the weakest assumption. Poke holes. Challenge the evidence. Then switch. | 25 min |
| **Post-Case Synthesis** | Full class. What did the discussions reveal? | 12 min |

> "If you've ever sat in a case discussion and watched a classmate's analysis fall apart under questioning, you know what good pressure-testing looks like. Today you're on both sides of that. You're defending your analysis AND interrogating someone else's."

### 2:25-2:40 | Case Prep: Identify Your Assumptions (15 min)

**Frame (1 min):**
> "Step one of any case analysis: what is the protagonist assuming? Except today, you're the protagonist. What are YOU assuming?"

> "Every recommendation rests on assumptions. In a written case, you spot them in the text. In your case, you have to surface them from your own thinking. That's harder, because you believe your own analysis."

Teams identify the assumptions their case depends on.

**Instructions:**
1. Write down at least 5 assumptions your recommendation depends on. These are things that must be true for your analysis to hold.
2. Categorize each:
   - **Desirability**: Do stakeholders actually want this? Will they change behavior?
   - **Feasibility**: Can this work in the real context?
   - **Viability**: Should an organization invest in this?
3. Rank: if this assumption is wrong, does the whole recommendation collapse, or just a piece of it?
4. Your top 2 should be the ones where the entire case falls apart if they're wrong.

**Facilitation notes:**
- Circulate. Push past surface-level assumptions ("people would use it"). Ask: "That's an adoption assumption. What about the deeper one? Is the problem you defined actually the right problem? What if the root cause is something else entirely?"
- Watch for teams that only list solution assumptions. Push them to examine their problem definition assumptions too. "What if your causal loop is missing a key variable? What if the feedback loop you identified isn't the dominant one?"
- If a team can only list 3, they haven't gone deep enough. Prompt: "What are you assuming about the stakeholder's behavior? About the organizational context? About the data you collected?"

### 2:40-2:55 | Decision Criteria: What Would Change Your Recommendation? (15 min)

**Frame (1 min):**
> "In a case discussion, the professor eventually asks: 'What would have to be true for you to change your recommendation?' That's this step. For your two most critical assumptions, define the evidence that would make you walk away."

For their top 2 assumptions, teams define kill criteria.

**Instructions:**
For your top 2 most critical assumptions:
1. What specific evidence would tell you this assumption is wrong?
2. Where would you find that evidence? Who would you ask? What would you observe?
3. How confident are you right now, and what is that confidence based on?

**The key distinction:**
- Decision criteria are specific and observable: "If more than 3 out of 5 stakeholders say they would never change this behavior, the recommendation fails."
- Not: "If people don't like it."

**Facilitation notes:**
- Push for "where would you find evidence?" This forces teams to think about testability.
- If a team says "we're very confident," ask: "Based on what? Which stakeholder told you that? How many?" Then: "Confidence without evidence is just optimism. In a board room, that gets you sent back to do more research."
- Some teams will resist ("but we believe in our idea"). Name it: "In a case discussion, conviction isn't evidence. The question is whether the data supports the conviction."

### 2:55-3:20 | Case Discussion: Pressure Test (25 min)

**Frame (2 min):**
> "Now the case discussion. In a traditional case class, the professor cold-calls you and challenges your analysis. Today, another team does that. Their job is the same as any good case discussant: find where the analysis is strong, find where it's weak, and push on the weak spots."

> "Presenting team: you're defending your case, but defend it honestly. If an assumption is weak, say so. The goal isn't to win the argument. It's to find out where your analysis needs work."

> "Questioning team: don't be polite. This is a case discussion, not a feedback session. If you see a gap between their evidence and their conclusion, name it."

**Setup:**
- Assign pairings (different scenarios preferred)
- Each pair gets 25 minutes total: ~12 min per team
- Timer visible on screen

**Instructions:**
1. Team A presents their case: the problem they defined, the recommendation they're making, and the two assumptions they think are most critical. (3 min)
2. Team B interrogates. Challenge the assumptions. Push on the evidence. Ask: "How do you know that? Who told you? What if the opposite is true?" (8-9 min)
3. Switch roles.

**Good case discussion questions to model:**
- "Your analysis assumes [X]. What evidence do you have for that beyond your own intuition?"
- "You interviewed [N] stakeholders. How do you know that's representative?"
- "Your causal loop shows [dynamic]. But what if [alternative dynamic] is actually driving this?"
- "If I'm the decision-maker and you bring me this recommendation, my first question is: why should I believe the problem is what you say it is?"
- "What's the strongest counterargument to your recommendation?"
- "You defined the problem as [X]. What did you choose NOT to include in that definition, and why?"

**Facilitation notes:**
- Circulate actively. This is the highest-value 25 minutes of the session.
- If teams are being too gentle: "That's a polite question, not a case discussion question. What's the assumption that worries you most about their analysis?"
- If the presenting team gets defensive: "You don't have to win this. Write down what you're hearing. The best case discussions reveal things the protagonist couldn't see."
- Watch for moments where the questioning team surfaces an assumption the presenting team didn't list. That's exactly what case discussions are for. Name it when it happens.
- The best discussions challenge the problem definition, not just the solution. If a team pushes back on whether the other team defined the right problem, that's gold.

### 3:20-3:32 | Post-Case Synthesis (12 min)

Bring the class back together.

**Frame:**
> "In a case class, the professor closes by naming what the discussion revealed. Let's do that."

**Discussion prompts:**
- "What did the case discussion reveal that your own case prep missed?"
- "Did anyone's case get STRONGER because of the discussion? How?"
- "Where was the gap between evidence and conviction widest?"
- "Did anyone get pushed on their problem definition, not just their solution? What happened?"

**Close the loop:**
> "Here's what just happened. You presented an analysis built from primary research, defended it under adversarial questioning, and found out where your evidence is strong and where you're running on assumption. That's a case discussion. The only difference is you wrote the case instead of reading it."

> "And here's the thing about writing your own case: you know where the data came from. You know which interviews were strong and which were thin. You know where you made interpretive leaps. When you read a Harvard case next semester, you'll read it differently, because you now know what the author chose to include and what they left out."

## Pressure Test: Does This Framing Hold?

### What works
- Students hear "case study" and immediately code this as MBA-level work
- The activities are identical to the assumption audit / stress test plan. Only the vocabulary changes.
- Positions their project work as MORE rigorous than a traditional case (they gathered primary data, the case author is in the room)
- The closing reframe ("you'll read Harvard cases differently now") gives them a narrative for why this course mattered

### Where it might break
- **Students might push back: "This isn't a real case study."** It doesn't have a teaching note, a protagonist decision point, or a Harvard Business Publishing header. If someone says this, the response is: "You're right, it's not a packaged case. It's the raw version. The version before someone cleaned it up and wrote the teaching note. You built the analysis that the case would be about."
- **Risk of over-promising the analogy.** A case discussion usually has a clear protagonist making a specific decision. The team projects may not have that sharp a decision point. Mitigation: frame it as "a case your team wrote" rather than "this IS a case study." The analogy should illuminate, not constrain.
- **Some teams may not have enough analytical depth to sustain the framing.** If a team's problem definition is thin, calling it a "case" highlights that thinness. Mitigation: this is actually a feature, not a bug. In a case discussion, weak analysis gets exposed. That's the point. And the session gives them time to find the gaps before Monday.
- **The "case discussion" framing might set up an expectation of cold-calling and Socratic questioning from you.** Students may expect you to play the traditional professor role (challenging each team). The paired format means teams challenge each other instead. Frame this explicitly: "In this case class, your classmates are the discussants."

### What to watch for in real time
- Does the "case" language land, or does it feel forced? If students engage naturally with the framing, lean in. If it feels like a stretch, you can soften to: "Think of this as your team presenting a case to a review panel."
- Are the paired discussions actually rigorous? The case framing raises the bar. If teams are still lobbing softballs, the framing isn't doing its job. Intervene: "In a case class, that question wouldn't survive. Go harder."
- Does the closing reframe ("you'll read cases differently") resonate? Watch their faces. If it lands, it's the best line in the session.
