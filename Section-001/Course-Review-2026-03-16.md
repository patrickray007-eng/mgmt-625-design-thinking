# MGMT 625 Section 001: Course Review Synthesis

**Date**: March 16, 2026
**Course**: Design Thinking, Rice Jones School of Business
**Semester**: Spring 2026 (12 sessions, Jan 21 to Mar 2, all sessions delivered)
**Sources**: Visual Design Panel, Practitioner Content Panel, Student Experience Panel

---

## Executive Summary

- **Sessions 7-11 are the intellectual core of the course** and the primary reason students would recommend it. The delayed ideation payoff (holding students in the problem space until Session 9) is the course's most distinctive and successful design choice, validated by all three panels.
- **Phase 3 is structurally compressed.** Four sessions (9-12) carry ideation, gallery walk, stress testing, prototyping, and final presentations. The prototype requirement lands in Session 11 with only a weekend before Session 12. All three panels flagged this independently.
- **Causal loop mapping is assigned but never taught in class.** This is the single highest-severity content gap, confirmed by both the Practitioner and Student panels and visible in the gallery walk quality variance.
- **The slide system has two distinct eras.** Sessions 1-4 have no HTML decks at all, Sessions 5-8 are one visual style, and Sessions 9-12 are another (with Lucide icons, custom components, and increasing density). Several slides have text too small for classroom projection.
- **Session 6 tries to do too much.** Stakeholder mapping, empathy interview training, and a Design Tapas menu with icebreaker-level activities crammed into 90 minutes. The tonal inconsistency was flagged by both the Student and Practitioner panels.

## Identified Shortcomings

### 1. Causal Loop Maps: Assigned Without In-Class Instruction
- **Severity**: Critical
- **Evidence**: Practitioner Panel (top content gap #1), Student Panel (complaint #2; Priya and James both struggled; gallery walk exposed preparation gap between teams)
- **Sessions affected**: 7 (assignment given), 8 (gallery walk and synthesis)
- **Recommendation**: Add a 15-minute worked example of building a causal loop map, either late in Session 7 or early in Session 8. The Session 8 slides teach how to *read* a map but not how to *construct* one. Even one live demonstration of identifying reinforcing/balancing loops from a team's stakeholder data would significantly improve output quality.

### 2. Phase 3 Time Compression (Sessions 9-12)
- **Severity**: Critical
- **Evidence**: Practitioner Panel (arc weakness: "aggressive" pacing from first ideas to tested prototype in 4 sessions), Student Panel (complaint #1; prototype-over-a-weekend problem; Session 12 has zero transition time between 7 teams)
- **Sessions affected**: 9, 10, 11, 12
- **Recommendation**: This is the biggest structural question for next semester. Options include: (a) shift the gallery walk earlier to free up a session for prototyping, (b) reduce team presentations from 9 to 7 minutes to build in transitions and Q&A, (c) introduce the prototype concept in Session 9 or 10 rather than Session 11. Detailed options in Next-Semester Recommendations below.

### 3. Session 6 Overloaded and Tonally Inconsistent
- **Severity**: High
- **Evidence**: Student Panel (complaint #5; James and Sofia flagged the Design Tapas menu as patronizing at Session 6; Priya noted the stakeholder-to-interview transition felt rushed), Practitioner Panel (Session 6 called "doing double duty")
- **Sessions affected**: 6
- **Recommendation**: Remove or relocate the Design Tapas menu from Session 6. Stakeholder mapping and empathy interview training are both substantial topics. Either split them across two sessions or cut the Tapas activities to give both topics adequate time. By Session 6, students do not need icebreakers.

### 4. Concept Selection Is Under-Taught
- **Severity**: High
- **Evidence**: Practitioner Panel (content gap #2: "strong on divergent generation but weak on convergent selection"), Student Panel (Sofia: "killing 7 of 9 ideas immediately wastes the divergent thinking"; James: hypothesis format time too short)
- **Sessions affected**: 9
- **Recommendation**: Add a brief concept selection framework (Desirability-Feasibility-Viability or similar) to Session 9. The DFV framework already appears in Sessions 10 and 11 but is introduced too late to guide the selection decision.

### 5. Prototyping Methods Are Barely Scaffolded
- **Severity**: High
- **Evidence**: Practitioner Panel (content gap #3: "the word 'prototype' appears repeatedly but prototyping is barely taught"), Student Panel (Sofia: no explicit connection between assumption audit and prototyping; Marcus: prototype requirement made the project feel real but was introduced late)
- **Sessions affected**: 11, 12
- **Recommendation**: Add concrete prototyping methods (paper prototypes, wizard of oz, role-played service encounters, storyboards) with examples at varying fidelity levels. One slide of examples in Session 9 or 10 would plant the seed earlier.

### 6. Session 1 Content Is Undocumented
- **Severity**: High
- **Evidence**: Practitioner Panel (content gap #5: "the most important session has no visible slide deck, no detailed activity instructions"), Visual Design Panel (Sessions 1-4 have no HTML decks)
- **Sessions affected**: 1
- **Recommendation**: Document the Design Tapas activity, the Session 1 slide content, and the actual flow as delivered. Without documentation, this session cannot be iterated on, shared with a co-instructor, or reviewed for improvement.

### 7. Slide Text Too Small for Projection Across Multiple Decks
- **Severity**: High
- **Evidence**: Visual Design Panel (systemic issue #1: compounding font-size reductions; worst case is Session 12 slide 6 at ~14px; Session 5 slide 4 timeline at ~14.5px; Session 6 slide 4 SVG text at 8px)
- **Sessions affected**: 5 (slide 4), 6 (slide 4), 9 (slide 5), 10 (slides 2, 5, 7), 12 (slides 4, 6)
- **Recommendation**: Establish a minimum effective font size of 18px for any text that students need to read. Audit all slides below this threshold. The tools table on Session 12 slide 6 needs to become a handout or multi-slide layout.

### 8. No Shared CSS File for Slide Decks
- **Severity**: Medium
- **Evidence**: Visual Design Panel (systemic issue #2: "styles are copied, pasted, and drift"; three different table header styles across seven decks; Lucide icons appear only in Sessions 9-12)
- **Sessions affected**: All HTML decks (5-6, 8-12)
- **Recommendation**: Extract shared CSS into a single file. This is a one-time effort that will prevent drift and make future edits much faster. Standardize table header styling and icon usage.

### 9. Session 5 Initial Team Work Block Is Under-Structured
- **Severity**: Medium
- **Evidence**: Practitioner Panel (Session 5 ranked #2 needing attention: "45 minutes of 'initial team work' with five discussion questions is understructured for a newly formed team"), Student Panel (James: "We hit a wall at 25 minutes"; Marcus: "assumptions question felt superficial")
- **Sessions affected**: 5
- **Recommendation**: Add intermediate structure to the 45-minute block: individual brainstorm (10 min) before group discussion, or a round-robin sharing format, plus a midpoint checkpoint.

### 10. Session 3 Debrief Too Short for Fieldwork Experience
- **Severity**: Medium
- **Evidence**: Practitioner Panel (Session 3 ranked #3 needing attention: "10-minute debrief is very short for unpacking what could be a disorienting experience"; "professional design research teams spend as much time debriefing as observing"), Student Panel (Priya: "I wanted more time to process with classmates")
- **Sessions affected**: 3
- **Recommendation**: Extend the debrief to at least 20 minutes. Consider shortening the fieldwork window or restructuring: 50 minutes fieldwork, 20 minutes debrief, 10 minutes naming.

### 11. Observation vs. Interpretation Never Explicitly Named
- **Severity**: Medium
- **Evidence**: Practitioner Panel (content gap #4: "foundational to everything that follows" but "never explicitly taught"; touched in Session 2 debrief but not anchored)
- **Sessions affected**: 2, 3
- **Recommendation**: Add a brief explicit teaching moment (2-3 minutes) in Session 2 or Session 3 that names the observation/interpretation distinction as a concept, not just a debrief question.

### 12. Information-Dense Slides Overflow or Clip
- **Severity**: Medium
- **Evidence**: Visual Design Panel (systemic issue #3: Session 9 slide 2, Session 10 slide 5, Session 11 slide 8, Session 12 slide 6 "pack far too much content into a single 1280x720 viewport")
- **Sessions affected**: 9, 10, 11, 12
- **Recommendation**: Split the four flagged slides into two slides each, or convert the densest (Session 12 slide 6, the tools table) to a handout.

### 13. Low-Contrast Text on Recurring Elements
- **Severity**: Low
- **Evidence**: Visual Design Panel (systemic issue #5: footer text #9CA3AF on white fails WCAG AA at ~2.9:1; tapas time labels; tic-tac-toe placeholder text)
- **Sessions affected**: All HTML decks
- **Recommendation**: Change footer text to at least #6B7280 for WCAG AA compliance. Fix the tic-tac-toe placeholder and tapas time label colors.

### 14. Mixed Image Sourcing Strategy in Later Decks
- **Severity**: Low
- **Evidence**: Visual Design Panel (Session 11 uses local + CDN images; Session 12 mixes local + OpenLibrary URLs; CDN dependency creates fragility for offline teaching)
- **Sessions affected**: 11, 12
- **Recommendation**: Standardize on local images for all decks. Download external images and store alongside the HTML files.

### 15. No Team Dynamics Support
- **Severity**: Low
- **Evidence**: Student Panel (complaint #4: "no explicit guidance on roles, decision-making, or conflict resolution"; Sofia flagged no labor division guidance)
- **Sessions affected**: 5-12
- **Recommendation**: Consider adding a brief (5-minute) team norms exercise to Session 5 after formation. This is a judgment call: the course is about design abilities, not team management, but minimal structure could prevent common friction points.

## Next-Semester Recommendations

### Structural Changes (High Priority)

**A. Decompress Phase 3 by introducing prototyping earlier.**

The current arc asks students to go from first ideas (Session 9) to tested prototype presentation (Session 12) in four sessions. Three options:

- **Option A1**: Move gallery walk to Session 9, making it a "problem definition showcase" rather than a mid-project check-in. This frees Session 10 for ideation and Session 11 for prototyping methods + stress test, giving teams a full week before Session 12.
- **Option A2**: Keep the sequence but introduce the prototype concept and methods in Session 9 alongside ideation, so teams have 3 sessions (not 1 weekend) to plan and execute prototyping.
- **Option A3**: Shorten final presentations from 9 to 7 minutes per team (saving ~14 minutes), and use recovered time for Q&A, transitions, and a longer reflection period.

**B. Add causal loop map instruction to Session 7.**

Reallocate 15 minutes from the HMW activity (currently 20 min) or the reframing activity (currently 25 min) to a live worked example of constructing a causal loop map. Walk through one team's stakeholder data and build a map together. Students then complete their own maps as homework with a concrete model to follow.

**C. Split Session 6 into focused halves or restructure.**

Option C1: Session 6 covers stakeholder mapping only. Move empathy interview training to a Canvas module or a 10-minute block in Session 7.
Option C2: Keep both topics but remove the Design Tapas menu entirely. Use the recovered time for deeper stakeholder mapping and a focused interview practice exercise.

### Content Additions (Medium Priority)

**D. Add concept selection framework to Session 9.**

After the Tic-Tac-Toe grid generates 9 ideas, give teams a structured way to evaluate and select. Introduce DFV (Desirability, Feasibility, Viability) as a selection lens rather than "pick your top 1-2." This also sets up the assumption audit in Session 11 where DFV categories are already used.

**E. Add prototyping methods reference.**

One slide or handout covering: paper prototypes, wizard of oz, storyboards, role-played service encounters, concierge tests. Include examples at different fidelity levels and guidance on matching fidelity to the assumption being tested. Place in Session 9 or 10 so teams have time to plan.

**F. Name observation vs. interpretation explicitly in Session 2.**

Add a 2-3 minute teaching moment after the debrief that names this as a foundational concept. "Observation ends where interpretation begins" already appears in the slides; elevate it to a deliberate teaching beat.

**G. Add peer evaluation to Session 12 final presentations.**

The gallery walk (Session 10) includes written peer feedback, but the final presentations (Session 12) do not. Add a simple feedback mechanism (even a single-question rating card per team) to the final session.

### Slide System Improvements (Medium Priority)

**H. Create a shared CSS file for all HTML decks.**

Extract the base theme (colors, typography, activity-box, callout-box, title slide, closing slide) into a single CSS file imported by all decks. This prevents drift and makes global changes (like fixing the contrast issues) a single edit.

**I. Establish and enforce a minimum text size.**

Set 18px effective minimum for any text students need to read. Audit all slides. Convert the Session 12 tools table (slide 6) to a handout or multi-slide format. Fix the Session 5 timeline and Session 6 SVG text sizes.

**J. Build HTML decks for Sessions 1-4.**

This gives the full course a consistent visual identity. Sessions 1-4 currently exist only as PDF (Session 1) or Slides-Instructions (Sessions 2-4). Building them into the shared reveal.js system completes the set.

**K. Standardize component usage across decks.**

Adopt Lucide icons from Session 5 onward (not just Session 9+). Pick one table header style and use it everywhere. Standardize on local images.

### Pedagogical Refinements (Lower Priority)

**L. Add structure to Session 5's 45-minute team work block.**

Introduce a 10-minute individual brainstorm before group discussion and a midpoint checkpoint at 25 minutes. This prevents the "hit a wall and go in circles" pattern flagged by James.

**M. Extend Session 3 debrief from 10 to 20 minutes.**

Shorten the fieldwork window from 60 to 50 minutes. The learning happens in the debrief, not just the observation. Professional design research teams invest heavily in debrief time.

**N. Add differentiated prompts for experienced designers.**

Sofia's feedback suggests that students with prior design experience can disengage in Sessions 1-4 and 6. Extension prompts or deeper challenge questions would maintain engagement without changing the core session for beginners.

**O. Make the Project 2 arc visible in Session 1.**

Marcus recommended showing the full arc (Design Abilities by Phase table) in Session 1 to help analytical students trust the pedagogy. This is a small change that could accelerate buy-in from skeptics.

## What's Working Well

These strengths should be preserved in any revision:

1. **The Explore-Make Sense-Name pattern.** Applied consistently across every session. All three panels validated it. Students who were initially skeptical (Marcus) came around by Session 4, and the experienced designer (Sofia) called it "a legitimate twist."

2. **Delayed ideation until Session 9.** The single most distinctive design choice in the course. The Practitioner Panel called it "what most design thinking courses get wrong." All four student panelists rated Session 9 engagement at 4 or 5 out of 5.

3. **Traceability as the throughline.** "No orphan solutions" and the demand that every idea trace back to research evidence separates this course from workshop-style design thinking. Sofia: "The best teams told stories that traced from a specific stakeholder moment to a systemic insight to a tested prototype."

4. **The activity box / callout box component system in the slides.** Dark-gray activity boxes for "do something now" and light-mint callout boxes for "instructor voice" are immediately legible and well-differentiated. Used consistently in every deck.

5. **Solutions Tic-Tac-Toe (Session 9).** All four student panelists flagged this as a highlight. James: "I'm taking this back to my team." Sofia: "Better than Crazy 8s because the axes force diversity." The Practitioner Panel called it "a genuinely original ideation tool."

6. **The stress test format (Session 11).** Adversarial-but-constructive peer challenge was the most valued activity in the course. James: "the session that makes the course worth taking." The "Case You Wrote" reframing (positioning student research as equivalent to a Harvard case) was called "a power move" by the Practitioner Panel.

7. **One action item between classes.** Managing cognitive load through restraint. The Practitioner Panel identified this as a sign of "real pedagogical maturity."

8. **Find the Duct Tape as recurring practice.** Training students to spot workarounds in daily life builds observational discipline outside the classroom. The Practitioner Panel noted it "transfers to professional life."

9. **The visual identity where it exists.** The core teal/navy/orange palette, Montserrat/Open Sans pairing, and component patterns are strong and recognizable. The Visual Design Panel noted "a student flipping between any two sessions would know they are in the same course."

10. **The 8th Ability ("Design Your Design Work") in Session 12.** The Practitioner Panel called it "a sophisticated move that gives students something to aspire to after the course ends." The machinist metaphor was singled out as memorable and grounding.

## Panel Disagreements

Two areas where panels offered different assessments:

- **Session 10 gallery walk format.** The Practitioner Panel rated it "Strong" and praised the docent protocol as mirroring professional practice. Marcus (Student Panel) found it chaotic and preferred structured presentations. The Visual Design Panel flagged slide 5 (the gallery walk reference) as severely overloaded. The format itself works, but the logistics (4.5 min per station with 7 teams) and the supporting materials need adjustment.

- **Session 2's remote video format.** James (Student Panel) found watching videos on Zoom to be a "hard sell" and gave it 3/5 engagement. Sofia (Student Panel) called it "better than most observation exercises I did in my UX bootcamp." The Practitioner Panel rated it "Strong." This is likely a personality/learning-style split rather than a design flaw. The remote format was weather-forced, not chosen, which limits how much to invest in optimizing it.
