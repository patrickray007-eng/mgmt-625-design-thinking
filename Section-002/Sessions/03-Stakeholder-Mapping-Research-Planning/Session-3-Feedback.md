# Session 3 Feedback — March 12, 2026

## Student Confusion: Causal Loop Scope

**What happened:** Students were genuinely lost about what their causal loop diagram should be about. The instructions never explicitly stated that the causal loop should map their team's chosen problem space from Session 2.

**Root cause:** The materials assumed students were carrying the mental model from Session 2 (problem selection → stakeholder mapping → causal loop → research). The slides taught causal loops using a worked example (99 Ranch Food Court), then jumped to the drafting activity with "sketch an initial causal loop map for your scenario" without a bridge statement connecting the example to their own work.

**Where the gap exists:**
- Session 3 slides introduce causal loops abstractly, demo with a different problem, then say "your scenario" without defining what that means
- No slide shows the through-line: Problem Selection (S2) → Stakeholder Mapping (S3) → Research Period → Causal Loop Map (S4)
- A04 assignment says "your team's problem" but only in the specs section, not in the "What to Do" intro
- Session 3 Overview uses "your scenario" once but never bridges to "the problem you chose in Session 2"

## Changes for Next Iteration

### Slides
- [ ] Add a bridge slide after the worked causal loop example: "Now apply this to YOUR team's problem from Session 2"
- [ ] Add a project arc visual early in the deck showing the full through-line (S2 → S3 → Research → S4)
- [ ] On the drafting activity slide, replace "your scenario" with "your team's problem from Session 2"
- [ ] Consider adding a "Where We Are in the Project" slide that explicitly names each team's scenario

### Assignment (A04)
- [ ] Open the "What to Do" section with: "Start from the problem your team selected in Session 2 and researched during the research period."
- [ ] Add explicit cross-reference to A03: "Your stakeholder research (A03) should inform your causal loop. The patterns you found in research reveal the dynamics to map."

### General Principle
- Never assume students carry the mental model from a prior session. State the connection explicitly, especially when switching from instruction/example to application.
- When using a worked example from a different context, always include a transition that says "now do this for YOUR problem."
