Prep: What Are You Assuming?
Team Activity
1. List at least 5 assumptions your recommendation depends on
2. Categorize each: Desirability, Feasibility, or Viability
3. Rank: if wrong, does everything collapse or just a piece?
4. Your top 2: the ones where everything falls apart
5. For those top 2: what observable evidence would prove you
wrong?
 ~10 minutes
 Desirability
Do stakeholders actually want this? Will they change
behavior?
 Feasibility
Can this work in the real context? Do the resources
exist?
 Viability
Should an organization invest in this? Is it sustainable?

Discussion: Pressure Test
Paired Teams
1. Team A briefs their case: problem, recommendation, two
critical assumptions. Conversational, not a presentation. (3
min)
2. Team B interrogates. Challenge assumptions. Push on
evidence. (8-9 min)
3. Switch roles.
 ~25 minutes total
Questions worth asking:
"What evidence do you have beyond your own intuition?"
"You interviewed [N] stakeholders. How do you know that's
representative?"
"What if [alternative dynamic] is actually driving this?"
"Why should I believe the problem is what you say it is?"
"What evidence would make you walk away from this
recommendation?"

Three Tools, One Company
Before Monday, you need to build a prototype. Here's what that means, and what it doesn't.
MVP
Built to Run
A product you ship and keep
running. It's rough, but it works
as a service. You grow it into a
business.
SURVIVAL TACTIC
Built to Not Die
Pure scrappiness. Whatever it
takes to keep the lights on and
prove you won't quit.
PROTOTYPE
Built to Learn
Tests one assumption with one
real person. Might be
disposable. Not built to last.
Built to find out if you're right.
You're building a prototype. Not an MVP. Not a business. A test.

The MVP: Air Mattresses, 2007
MVP
Chesky and Gebbia can't make rent. Design conference in SF,
hotels sold out. They buy three air mattresses, build a simple
website: AirBed and Breakfast. $80/night. Breakfast included.
Three strangers show up and pay.
They kept running it. Kept hosting.
It became the business.

The Survival Tactic: Cereal Boxes, 2008
SURVIVAL
One year later. Airbnb is nearly dead. No funding, no traction,
massive debt. The 2008 election is happening.
They design custom cereal: Obama O's and Cap'n McCains. 500
boxes each. $20 to make, $40 to sell. They sell out. $30,000.
Enough to keep the lights on.
Paul Graham, Y Combinator:
"If you can convince people to pay $40 for a $4 box of cereal,
you can convince people to stay in each other's homes. You
are cockroaches. You will not die."

The Prototype: Photography, 2009
PROTOTYPE
Airbnb has users, but nobody's booking. Hypothesis: the photos
are terrible and that's killing trust.
They rent a camera, fly to New York, knock on hosts' doors. One
week. One apartment at a time.
2-3x more bookings.
Revenue doubled that month.
Then they threw away the prototype
and built the real program.
"A web startup would say, 'Let's send emails, teach users
professional photography, and test them.' We said, 'Screw
that.'"
They rented a $5,000 camera and snapped high-resolution
photos of as many New York host apartments as they
could. Bookings soared. By month's end, revenue had
doubled.
"When we fixed the product in New York, it solved our
problems in Paris, London, Vancouver, and Miami."
Chesky & Gebbia, Fast Company

Studio Time: Plan Your Prototype
Team Activity: Answer three questions. Write them down.
1. Which assumption are you testing?
Pick your riskiest one from the case discussion.
2. What's the smallest thing you can build to test it?
Not the whole solution. Just the test.
3. Who will you test it with, and when?
Name a real person. Pick a day before Monday.
 ~15 minutes
A paper mockup. A role-played service encounter. A structured conversation. A one-page document in front of a
decision-maker. It can be rough. A real person has to interact with it.
