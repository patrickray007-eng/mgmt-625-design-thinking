Design Thinking Notebook

Assignment Overview

Your notebook is the artifact of your design thinking practice throughout this course. It contains your observations, sketches, maps, reflections, and in-class activities, the raw material of your learning journey.

Unlike polished deliverables, the notebook values process over presentation. It should show evidence of sustained engagement, not perfect documentation.

Learning Objectives

  • Maintain a continuous practice of documentation throughout the design process
  • Capture observations, ideas, and reflections in real-time
  • Create a personal reference for design thinking practices
  • Develop the habit of externalizing thinking

Instructions

Required Entries

These are the core notebook components that will be assessed:

TETHER Observation

  • Full 90-minute observation notes
  • Assumptions noted in margins
  • Reflection on the experience

Find the Duct Tape (Weekly, throughout the course)

  • Each week, identify and document one "duct tape" solution you encounter: a workaround, patch, or improvised fix that people use to deal with a broken process or poorly designed system
  • Record: What is the workaround? What problem is it solving? Why does the "real" fix not exist?
  • These entries accumulate throughout the course and should appear regularly in your notebook

Individual Research Notes (Research Period, Sessions 3-4)

  • Individual observation/interview notes from your Stakeholder Research
  • Raw research data: observation logs, interview notes, key quotes
  • Insights and surprises
  • These notes are your individual research record and will be assessed as part of your notebook grade

Stakeholder Map Drafts

  • Working drafts of stakeholder maps from your team project

Causal Loop Drafts

  • First drafts and iterations of your causal loop map

Problem Definition Iterations

  • Working drafts of problem statements showing how your thinking evolved

Optional: In-Class Notes

In-class notes are not required, but including them can be a helpful way to demonstrate engagement throughout the course. Examples of what you might capture:

  • Activity notes (ambiguity exercises, AEIOU framework, observation activities)
  • Ideation outputs (problem framing attempts, hypothesis drafts)
  • Feedback received from peers during presentations or stress testing
  • Notes from other teams' presentations
  • Reflections on what you learned in a given session

Format

  • Physical notebook: Submit photos or scans (one PDF, pages in order)
  • Digital notebook: Submit as PDF with clear organization by session/activity

What We're Looking For

This is graded on completion and sustained engagement, not on neatness or "correct" answers.

  • Evidence that you used the notebook throughout the course (not filled in the night before)
  • Entries from each phase of the course
  • Your actual thinking. Messy is fine, empty is not

Deliverables

Submit to Canvas before Session 6 (Thursday, April 9):

  • Complete notebook (PDF, scanned pages or digital document)
  • Pages in chronological order
  • Clearly legible (doesn't need to be neat, but must be readable)

Grading Rubric (Specifications-Based)

This assignment is graded on a Meets Specifications / Revise basis.

Specification 1: TETHER Documentation

Specification Met Specification Not Met
Notebook includes substantial TETHER observation notes (3-4+ pages). Notes reflect 90 minutes of sustained observation. Assumptions are noted. Reflection is present. TETHER section is missing, sparse, or clearly rushed. Fewer than 2 pages of observation notes. No assumptions noted. No reflection.

Specification 2: Session Coverage

Specification Met Specification Not Met
Notebook includes entries from each major course phase: Session 1 (ambiguity), Session 2 (observation/team formation), Session 3 (stakeholder mapping), research period, Session 4 (synthesis), Session 5 (presentations/stress test). Not every activity needs extensive notes, but each phase is represented. Multiple phases are missing entirely. Notebook only covers 1-2 sessions. Large gaps in documentation suggest the notebook wasn't used throughout the course.

Specification 3: Sustained Engagement

Specification Met Specification Not Met
Entries show evidence of real-time documentation (not reconstructed later). Notes include context-specific details that wouldn't be remembered later. Handwriting/formatting varies naturally across sessions. Content couldn't have been created in one sitting. Notebook appears to have been filled in all at once. Entries lack specific details. Formatting is suspiciously consistent throughout. Content is generic enough to have been created without attending sessions.

Specification 4: Thinking Made Visible

Specification Met Specification Not Met
Notebook shows actual thinking: drafts, iterations, questions, dead ends. Includes stakeholder maps, causal loop attempts, ideation sketches, or other visual thinking. Evidence of working through ideas, not just recording final answers. Notebook contains only final, polished outputs copied from other deliverables. No evidence of drafting or iteration. No visual thinking (maps, sketches, diagrams). Reads like a transcript, not a thinking tool.

Specification 5: Legibility and Organization

Specification Met Specification Not Met
Notebook is legible (doesn't need to be neat, but can be read). Pages are in chronological order. Clear enough organization to identify which entries correspond to which activities. Illegible or unreadable sections. Pages out of order or disorganized. Cannot determine what entries correspond to what activities.

Overall: Assignment meets specifications when ALL FIVE specifications are met. If any specification is not met, the assignment should be revised and resubmitted.

Note

The notebook is your artifact, not a performance. We're looking for evidence that you engaged with the work, not for beautiful documentation. Messy thinking captured in real-time is more valuable than polished notes created after the fact.
