Stakeholder Mapping

Design Thinking for MBAs
Monday, February 9, 2026  |  Instructor: Patrick Ray

Who Is Affected by This Problem?

With your team, identify 3-4 stakeholders affected by your problem scenario.

Include at least one extreme user: someone who experiences this problem 10x more intensely than average, or who has found creative workarounds.

For each stakeholder, document:

  1. Who are they? (Be specific: "frontline nurses," not "healthcare workers")
  2. What do they optimize for?
  3. What constraints do they face?
~20 minutes

Stakeholder Mapping Template

Element Prompt
Who Specific role or identity (not generic)
Optimizes for What are they trying to maximize?
Constraints What limits their options?
Top concerns What do they care about most? (2-3)
Tensions Where might their interests conflict with others?
"If everyone on your list wants the same thing, that's a clue you might need to reapproach."

Visual Examples

Concentric Circles

Map stakeholders by proximity to the problem. Who feels it most directly?

2x2 Matrix

Plot by influence and interest. Who has the power to change things?

Go Deeper

Choose your 2-3 most critical stakeholders and explore:

  1. What are their top 2-3 concerns?
  2. What do they care about most?
  3. What would they sacrifice to get what they want?
  4. Where do their concerns intersect with other stakeholders?
  5. What are some cause/effect relationships between stakeholders?

Look for tensions. If solving for one stakeholder creates a problem for another, you're on to something.

~20 minutes

What Did You Find?

  • Name one stakeholder your team almost forgot. Why were they easy to overlook?
  • Where do stakeholder concerns conflict?
  • Which stakeholders are hardest to access?
"You've mapped these stakeholders on paper. Now you need to learn from them directly."

Activity: The "Why?" Interview

Round 1 (2 min each)

Interview your partner about a topic (e.g., their morning routine) using only body language and nodding. No words. You can ask only the initial question.

Round 2 (2 min each)

Same topic. Now you can ask "Why?"

"Notice how much deeper the conversation goes when you have just one word to work with."

Reflection: The "Why?" Interview

  • How did that feel? What was challenging?
  • When did the conversation get interesting?
  • What surprised you about what you heard?
  • How did silence change the dynamic?

Empathy Interview Principles

  • Listen more than you talk. Your job is to understand their world, not explain yours.
  • Follow curiosity. When something surprises you, go deeper: "Tell me more about that."
  • Ask for stories, not opinions. "Tell me about a time when..." beats "What do you think about..."
  • Embrace silence. After they answer, wait. They'll often fill the silence with something more interesting.
  • No leading questions. "How do you experience X?" not "What's frustrating about X?"
