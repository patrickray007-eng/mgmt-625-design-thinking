Coursecorrection

Result: Meets Specifications

All five specifications met. Your problem statement is well-structured: feedback "fails to translate into meaningful course improvement because feedback is collected too late, perceived as low-stakes, and designed in ways that produce weak or distorted signals." The three-part "because" clause gives the problem structure. The professor's quote about six-week courses not having "enough time to gather impressions and implement changes" and the student going directly to the professor when "something really bothers me" show you're seeing the formal system's failure from multiple angles.

Looking Ahead to the Final Presentation

  1. Your stakeholders want different things, and your solution currently favors one of them. Students want "quick visible improvements." Professors "work on longer timelines and need actionable detail." Admins "want consistency for benchmarking." Your voting platform is designed around the student experience (suggest → upvote → see results), but you haven't shown how it serves the professor who needs actionable detail or the admin who needs benchmarking data. For the final, show how the same platform delivers value to all three, or be honest that you're prioritizing one stakeholder and explain why.
  1. Confront the participation problem. Your own research found that students rush evaluations, perceive them as low-stakes, and default to informal channels when something really matters. What makes your platform different? If students already feel "no one really looks at it," why would they invest time in a new feedback tool? The voting mechanism is interesting, but you need to name the specific mechanism that breaks the cycle of apathy. Is it the visibility of implementation status? The peer signal from upvotes? Ground it in something you heard from students.
  1. Test the concept, not just the problem. Your feedback question ("What context would make ratings more fair or useful?") is still exploring the problem space. You're past that stage. For the final, the question should be: would students actually use this? Would professors respond mid-semester? Show your platform concept to a professor or student and get a reaction. That kind of stakeholder testing will matter more than refining the feature set.

Result: Meets Specifications

Looking Ahead to the Final Presentation

  1. Your two directions may be in tension with each other. Direction 1 says the problem is signal credibility (managers distrust internally generated data), so bring in an impartial third party. Direction 2 says the problem is signal quality (feedback comes from the wrong customers), so weight input from high-value regulars. These diagnose different root causes. Is the issue that managers distrust who's giving feedback, or that the wrong people are giving it? For the final, clarify whether these are complementary interventions or competing hypotheses. If complementary, show how they work together. If competing, pick the one your evidence supports more strongly.
  1. Direction 2 has an equity problem you should address head-on. You already flagged it: "How do you identify and prioritize high-value customers without creating a tiered experience that makes others feel excluded?" That's not an implementation detail. A fast-food chain that visibly treats regulars' feedback as more important could create exactly the customer resentment your research already documents. For the final, either show how this works without a two-tier system, or explain why the tradeoff is worth it.
  1. Push past the CFA finding into a specific design. The third-party audit proves "quiet signal detection is possible," but CFA outsources it rather than building it into daily workflows. You note the question is "how to make it continuous rather than periodic." That's the design challenge for the final. Your observation that staff fix 15+ issues per shift without documenting them is the starting point. What would it take to capture even a fraction of those corrections? What did employees say when you asked them about logging fixes during high-volume periods? You listed this as something you "don't know yet." For the final, go find out.
