> [!abstract]- Course Overview
>
> ## The Premise
>
> Every one of you will face problems where the case study doesn't exist yet. Where the data is ambiguous, stakeholders disagree, and the "right answer" depends on questions nobody has thought to ask. This course gives you a structured process for those moments. It's called design thinking, but what it really is: a discipline for leading through ambiguity.
>
> This is not New Enterprises. We're not building businesses. We're building the observational and analytical muscles you need before you can build anything.
>
> ## The Analytical Arc
>
> The course follows a deliberate research methodology:
>
> 1. **Primary research methods** (Sessions 1-2): Ethnographic fieldwork, structured observation, stakeholder identification
> 2. **Systems modeling** (Sessions 3-4): Stakeholder mapping, causal loop analysis, root cause identification
> 3. **Problem definition** (Session 4): Evidence-based problem framing, boundary-setting, tension analysis
> 4. **Hypothesis generation and testing** (Sessions 5-6): Solution hypotheses, assumption audits, adversarial review
>
> Each phase has specific analytical tools. Each deliverable asks you to show your reasoning.
>
> ## 7 Professional Competencies (Design Abilities)
>
> These are the capabilities the course builds. Each one maps directly to leadership situations you will face.
>
> | Competency | What It Means | When an MBA Needs It |
> |------------|---------------|----------------------|
> | **Navigate Ambiguity** | Persist through incomplete information without forcing premature closure | Leading a team through a reorg when nobody knows what the new structure looks like |
> | **Learn From Others** | Gather primary data from diverse stakeholders through observation and inquiry | Entering a new market where your assumptions about the customer are wrong |
> | **Synthesize Information** | Find patterns and insight across disparate data sources | Distilling 40 customer interviews into three actionable themes |
> | **Experiment Rapidly** | Generate and test multiple hypotheses quickly | Your CEO wants a recommendation by Friday and you have three possible approaches |
> | **Move Between Concrete and Abstract** | Zoom between ground-level details and systems-level patterns | Connecting a frontline complaint to a structural incentive problem |
> | **Build and Craft Intentionally** | Show work at the right resolution for the feedback you need | Presenting a rough concept to a board vs. a polished deck to investors |
> | **Communicate Deliberately** | Tell the story of your analytical process, not just your conclusion | Making a recommendation where the reasoning matters as much as the answer |
>

> [!note]- Pedagogical Approach
>
> ## Inquiry-Based Learning
>
> This course uses an **Explore, Make Sense, Name** pattern:
> - **Explore:** You dive into activities before full scaffolding is provided.
> - **Make Sense:** Surface discoveries, struggles, and patterns collectively.
> - **Name:** Introduce vocabulary and frameworks after experience.
>
> This sequence is intentional. Research on expertise development shows that struggling with a problem before receiving instruction produces deeper, more transferable learning than lecture-first approaches. If it feels uncomfortable, that discomfort is evidence that the pedagogy is working.
>
> ## Making the Rigor Visible
>
> Every activity in this course follows a **Frame / Do / Debrief** structure:
>
> - **Frame** (before activity): "The skill we're building is [X]. In management, this matters because [Y]. Here's what I'll be looking for."
> - **Do** (activity): The Explore, Make Sense, Name pedagogy runs as designed.
> - **Debrief** (after activity): "What you just did was [analytical move]. In consulting / product development / organizational change, this is the step where [real-world anchor]."
>
> ## The Notebook
>
> Each learner maintains a design thinking notebook (physical or digital). Contains: Find the Duct Tape entries (weekly), ethnographic observation notes, stakeholder maps, causal loops, and reflections. Submitted digitally at course end. Graded on completion and sustained engagement.
>
> ## Distributed Research as Strength
>
> The two-week research gap (Mar 12-26) is intentional. Teams leverage members' diverse environments (different workplaces, industries, geographic contexts) to gather richer stakeholder data than any single location could provide.
>
> ## Asynchronous + Synchronous Balance
>
> Online sessions focus on framing, synthesis, and team coordination. In-person sessions carry hands-on activities, team formation, and presentations. Fieldwork happens asynchronously in students' own contexts.

> ### Choosing Your Subject
>
> A **scenario** is a broad problem space. A **subject** is your team's specific investigation within it.
>
> **Examples:**
> - Scenario A (First 90 Days) + Subject: "How do new nurses navigate their first rotation at a community hospital?"
> - Scenario D (Access Barriers) + Subject: "Why do eligible families in one school district not use the free tutoring program?"
> - Scenario B (Small Business Friction) + Subject: "What operational workarounds do independent coffee shop owners rely on daily?"
>
> **How to choose well:**
> - Pick a subject where your team can access real stakeholders (you need to observe and talk to people, not just read about them)
> - Keep it bounded: one organization, one location, or one population
> - Follow genuine curiosity, not what seems "important"
> - Aim for no more than 3 stakeholder groups
>
> **The hybrid advantage:** Your team members work in different industries, live in different cities, and move through different communities. That combined reach is your research superpower. Choose a subject where your team's diverse contexts give you access to different facets of the problem.

> ### A01: TETHER Observation
>
> **Deliverable:** 90-minute structured observation with raw field notes (3-4+ pages) and half-page reflection.
> **Scope:** Individual. Completed asynchronously before Session 2.
> **Due:** Session 2 (Saturday, March 7). Submitted as part of Notebook at Session 6.
> **Graded as:** Part of Notebook (10%).
>
> **Why this matters:** Ethnographic observation is the foundation of primary research. Consultants, product managers, and organizational leaders who can observe a system without immediately diagnosing it produce better analyses. This assignment trains that discipline.
>
> **What to do:**
> 1. Choose a location in your own environment where you can observe people interacting with systems, spaces, or each other for 90 uninterrupted minutes.
> 2. Bring only your notebook and a pen. Phone on silent and put away.
> 3. Document what you see, not what you think it means. Record movements, pauses, interactions, objects, and how people use the space. Include timestamps every 15-20 minutes.
> 4. Flag assumptions separately in the margins. When you catch yourself interpreting, note it.
> 5. Write a half-page reflection (~250 words): What was difficult? What did you notice about your attention? What surprised you?
>
> **Specifications:**
> 1. **Observation Duration and Depth:** Observations reflect 90 minutes of sustained attention, span 3-4+ pages, include multiple observation types, and show timestamps throughout.
> 2. **Descriptive vs. Interpretive:** Observations focus on observable behaviors without inferring motivation. Interpretations are flagged as assumptions.
> 3. **Assumption Awareness:** At least 3-5 specific assumptions identified in margins, demonstrating metacognitive awareness.
> 4. **Reflection Quality:** ~250 words addressing difficulty, attention patterns, surprises, and future adjustments with specific examples.

> ### A04: Causal Loop Map (Systems Model)
>
> **Deliverable:** Causal loop diagram (image or PDF) with half-page written explanation.
> **Scope:** Team.
> **Due:** Session 4 (Thursday, March 26).
> **Weight:** 5%.
>
> **Why this matters:** Causal loop mapping is the same systems modeling tool used in organizational diagnosis, supply chain analysis, and policy design. It forces you to move beyond "this is a problem" to "here is why this problem persists, who benefits from it, and where intervention would actually work." That analytical move, from symptom to system, is one of the most valuable skills in management.
>
> **What to do:**
> 1. Start with your core problem at the center. Be specific (not "communication issues" but "frontline staff feedback doesn't reach decision-makers").
> 2. Identify contributing factors and draw arrows toward the problem.
> 3. Trace effects and draw arrows away from the problem.
> 4. Find the loops. Where do effects become causes? Label with R (reinforcing) or B (balancing). You need at least 2 feedback loops.
> 5. Mark relationships with +/- signs.
> 6. Identify who benefits from the current state and why they might resist change.
> 7. Write a half-page explanation of the most important dynamics and how they connect to your stakeholder research.
>
> Your map should include at least 8-10 nodes, at least 2 feedback loops, and annotations for at least 2 loops plus 1 "who benefits" insight. Draft your map in your notebook first (notebook drafts are assessed separately).
>
> **Specifications:**
> 1. **System Complexity:** 8-10+ distinct nodes specific to your scenario, with plausible relationships grounded in research.
> 2. **Feedback Loop Identification:** At least 2 loops identified, labeled (R or B), and explained.
> 3. **Relationship Clarity:** Arrows clearly show direction with +/- signs. A reader can trace the system dynamics.
> 4. **"Who Benefits" Analysis:** Identifies at least one stakeholder who benefits from the problem persisting and explains why.
> 5. **Explanation Quality:** Half-page explanation connects the map to stakeholder research and illuminates why the dynamics matter.
>

> ### A05: Problem Definition
>
> **Deliverable:** Problem definition document (PDF) with five required sections.
> **Scope:** Team.
> **Due:** Session 5 (Saturday, April 4), before class.
> **Weight:** 10%.
>
> **Why this matters:** Defining the right problem is the highest-leverage analytical move in any project. McKinsey's first step in any engagement is problem definition, not solution generation. A well-framed problem statement that traces to evidence prevents the most common failure mode in strategy and innovation: solving the wrong problem brilliantly.
>
> **What to do:**
> 1. Write a problem statement identifying who experiences the problem, what it is (observable, not assumed), how it affects stakeholders, why it matters, and what's in/out of scope.
> 2. Summarize your evidence: key findings from stakeholder research, insights from causal loop analysis, how different team members' research contexts contributed.
> 3. Analyze stakeholder tensions: where interests conflict, what trade-offs exist, who would need to change behavior.
> 4. Propose 2-3 solution hypotheses framed as testable statements: "We believe that [solution] will [outcome] for [stakeholder] because [insight from research]." Identify the riskiest assumption for each.
> 5. Create a traceability map (simple visual) showing how hypotheses trace back to specific stakeholder insights, causal loop dynamics, and observed problems.
>
> **Specifications:**
> 1. **Problem Statement Quality:** Identifies specific stakeholders, an observable problem, and clear scope. Could guide solution development.
> 2. **Evidence Grounding:** Includes specific findings from research (quotes, observations). Clear connection between research and definition.
> 3. **Stakeholder Tension Analysis:** Identifies genuine tensions. Explains why the problem persists and why solutions may face resistance.
> 4. **Hypothesis Quality:** 2-3 testable hypotheses linked to research insights with riskiest assumptions identified. Hypotheses are distinct approaches.
> 5. **Traceability:** Visual shows clear logic chain from research to insights to problem to hypotheses.
> 6. **Document Completeness:** All five sections present and complete.
>

> ### A06: Final Presentation
>
> **Deliverable:** 8-10 minute team presentation with 4-5 minute adversarial review (Q&A and stress testing).
> **Scope:** Team. Upload slides to Canvas after session.
> **Due:** Session 5 (Saturday, April 4), presented in class.
> **Weight:** 25%.
>
> **Why this matters:** Presenting an evidence-based recommendation under pressure is the core deliverable of consulting, product management, and strategic leadership. This is not a pitch. It's a demonstration that your team can trace a line from primary research through analysis to recommendation, and defend that reasoning when challenged.
>
> **What to do:**
> 1. **Present the problem (2-3 min).** Who experiences it, why it matters, what makes it hard to solve (tensions, feedback loops from your systems model).
> 2. **Show your research journey (3-4 min).** How your team leveraged distributed fieldwork. 2-3 concrete examples (quotes, observations, moments that shifted your understanding).
> 3. **Present your solution hypotheses (2-3 min).** 1-2 solution concepts, how each connects to specific research insights, key assumptions, what you'd test first with more time.
> 4. **Adversarial review (4-5 min).** Classmates will pressure-test your logic, identify missed assumptions, and surface alternative explanations. It's fine to say "we don't know, that's an assumption we'd need to test."
>
> Use your traceability map. Favor visuals (stakeholder maps, causal loops, research photos) over text-heavy slides.
>
> **Specifications:**
> 1. **Problem Clarity:** Problem is clearly articulated. Audience understands who, what, and why. Bounded scope with explanation of persistence.
> 2. **Research Evidence:** 2-3 concrete examples from fieldwork. Demonstrates how distributed research added value.
> 3. **Traceability:** Clear logic chain from research to insights to problem to hypotheses.
> 4. **Assumption Awareness:** Key assumptions identified. Team acknowledges what they don't know.
> 5. **Presentation Delivery:** 8-10 minutes, all members contribute, organized, visual aids support the narrative.
> 6. **Engagement with Feedback:** Team engages constructively with adversarial review. Distinguishes evidence from assumptions.

> [!quote]- Appendix A: TETHER Observation Guide
>
> **T**ake **E**xtended **T**ime to **H**old **E**verything in **R**eview
>
> ### Setup
> - Choose a location in your own environment where you can observe for 90 uninterrupted minutes
> - Good options: workplace common area, local coffee shop, grocery store, transit station, park
> - Arrive with notebook and pen only. No laptop, phone on silent and away
>
> ### During Observation
> - Document what you see, not what you think it means
> - Note: movements, pauses, interactions, objects used, spaces occupied
> - Resist the urge to interpret or solve
> - When you catch yourself making assumptions, note them separately in the margins
> - Include timestamps every 15-20 minutes
>
> ### After Observation
> - Write a half-page reflection: What was hard? What did you notice about your own attention? What surprised you?
>
> ### The Hybrid Advantage
> Your observation location is unique to you. The diversity of contexts across the class creates richer collective primary data than any single campus location could provide.

## Key Differences from Original Plan

> [!info]- Changes Log (Section 001 Feedback Integration)
>
> This section documents what changed from the original Course-Plan.md and why.
>
> ### Framing Changes
> 1. **Course opens with leadership frame, not design frame.** "Every one of you will face problems where the case study doesn't exist yet." Addresses "should be an ILE" and "not MBA-level" feedback.
> 2. **Analytical arc shown in Session 1.** Students see the full research methodology up front: primary research, systems modeling, problem definition, hypothesis testing. Addresses "organization" and "can't see the rigor" complaints.
> 3. **Design Abilities renamed as Professional Competencies** with MBA-context examples. Same abilities, translated into language that signals rigor.
> 4. **"Not New Enterprises" line delivered early.** Preempts the "startup class" misconception.
> 5. **Business vocabulary used for all design moves.** Empathy becomes stakeholder research. Observation becomes ethnographic fieldwork. Ideation becomes hypothesis generation. Same work, vocabulary signals analytical rigor.
>
> ### Structural Changes
> 6. **Every activity uses Frame / Do / Debrief structure.** Opens with "the skill we're building is [X], in management this matters because [Y]." Closes with "what you just did was [analytical move], in [professional context] this is where [anchor]." Addresses "activities don't clearly connect" feedback.
> 7. **Assignment descriptions lead with deliverable and scope.** What, how long, individual or team, due date all appear before context or specs. Addresses "instructions are confusing" feedback.
> 8. **"Why This Matters" line added to every assignment.** Connects each deliverable to a professional competency.
>
> ### Policy Changes
> 9. **Token system replaced with simple extension policy.** One free 24-hour extension per person, email before deadline. Addresses "token system is confusing" feedback.
> 10. **Quiet signal replaced.** "Let's regroup, 30 seconds to wrap your thought" + visible timer during group work. Addresses "arms-up signal feels infantilizing" feedback.
> 11. **Q&A discipline codified.** Procedural questions get direct answers. Socratic method reserved for conceptual questions.
> 12. **Laptop policy explained with rationale and flexibility.** Closed during observation/attention activities, open during synthesis and team work. Addresses "no-laptop policy disliked" feedback.
>
> ### Preserved Elements
> - Same session schedule and hybrid format
> - Same project structure (Project 1 individual, Project 2 team)
> - Same assignment sequence and weights
> - Explore, Make Sense, Name pedagogy (now wrapped in Frame/Do/Debrief)
> - Specifications-based grading
> - The intentional ambiguity of project scenarios
> - Video ambiguity exercise in Session 1
> - TETHER observation
> - Find the Duct Tape weekly practice
> - Distributed research as pedagogical advantage
