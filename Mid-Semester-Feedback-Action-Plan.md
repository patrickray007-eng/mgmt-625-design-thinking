## Reframed Diagnosis

Students said "organization." But the assignments are on Canvas, slides go up after class, and the schedule is posted. What they're actually experiencing is: **the in-class time feels unstructured, and they can't see the rigor.**

Design thinking pedagogy is intentionally experiential. Explore → Make Sense → Name. But if students go through activities without seeing the analytical framework underneath, they code it as "disorganized" or "not MBA-level." The duct tape exercise, the observation work, the causal loop mapping: these ARE rigorous analytical tools. But students are comparing the experience to case method classes where the intellectual weight is more visible.

**The real fix isn't more Canvas posts. It's making the rigor visible in the room.**

The student who wanted to "bridge individual contributor design process with MBA-level analytical thinking" actually named the gap perfectly. They're doing the analytical work. They just don't recognize it as analytical because it doesn't feel like a traditional MBA class.

---

## The Rigor Problem (and how to address it)

### What's happening

Students see:
- Activities that feel playful or informal
- Ambiguity that feels like lack of preparation (rather than intentional pedagogy)
- A project that feels like "just go interview people" rather than structured research
- No visible connection to frameworks, models, or analytical depth

What's actually there:
- Systems thinking (causal loop maps)
- Stakeholder analysis
- Structured problem framing with evidence requirements
- Assumption testing and falsification
- Traceability from empathy research through to solutions

### Making rigor visible: Session-level moves

**Bookend every activity with explicit framing.** Not just "today we're doing X" but:
- **Open**: "The skill we're building right now is [specific Design Ability]. In management, this matters because [connection to leadership/strategy]. Here's what I'll be looking for."
- **Close**: "What you just did was [name the analytical move]. In a consulting engagement / product launch / organizational redesign, this is the step where [real-world anchor]."

**Name the methodology, not just the activity.** Instead of "let's do peer feedback," say "This is an assumption audit. In product development, teams that skip this step build solutions to problems that don't exist. You're learning to falsify your own hypotheses before investing resources."

**Connect backward explicitly.** "Your causal loop map is a systems model. That's the same analytical tool McKinsey uses when they map organizational dysfunction. The difference is you built yours from primary research, not secondary data."

**Use business language for design moves.** Students hear "empathy" and think soft skills. Reframe:
- Empathy → stakeholder research / primary data collection
- Observation → ethnographic fieldwork
- Problem framing → root cause analysis
- Ideation → hypothesis generation
- Prototyping → minimum viable experiment
- Peer feedback → adversarial review

This isn't dumbing it down. It's translating. The work is the same; the vocabulary signals rigor.

## Section 001: Final 2 Sessions (Feb 25, Mar 2)

Today (Session 10) is already happening. For the remaining two:

### Session 11 (Wed Feb 25): Assumption Audit + Stress Test

This session is actually the most MBA-flavored session in the course. Lean into that.

- Open by framing assumption testing as a core analytical discipline: "Every business plan, every strategy, every product decision rests on assumptions. Most of them are untested. Today you're learning to name yours and try to kill them before the market does."
- When teams identify their 5 critical assumptions, push them to distinguish between **desirability assumptions** (do people want this?), **feasibility assumptions** (can this work?), and **viability assumptions** (should an organization do this?). That's a d.school/IDEO framework that maps directly to MBA thinking.
- During the adversarial Q&A, give the audience explicit permission to be tough: "Your job is to find the weakest assumption. If you can break it in 3 minutes, imagine what a stakeholder will do."

### Session 12 (Mon Mar 2): Final Presentations

- Before presentations begin, briefly name what you're evaluating and why: "I'm looking for traceability. Can I follow the thread from what you observed, to what you learned, to what you built? That's the difference between a solution that's grounded in evidence and one that's just a good guess."
- After presentations, close the course by naming what they've built: "You now have a structured process for approaching problems you don't understand yet. That's not something most MBA programs teach explicitly. Most of your career will be spent on problems where the case hasn't been written yet."

## Section 002: Build rigor in from day one (starts Feb 26)

### Session 1 (Thu Feb 26): Course Intro

This is where the framing matters most. Set the tone in the first 15 minutes.

- [ ] **Open with the leadership frame, not the design frame.** Something like: "Every one of you will face problems in your career where the case study doesn't exist yet. Where the data is ambiguous, the stakeholders disagree, and the 'right answer' depends on questions nobody has thought to ask. This course gives you a structured process for those moments. It's called design thinking, but what it really is: a discipline for leading through ambiguity."
- [ ] **Show the analytical arc up front.** Put the course structure on screen and walk through it: "We start with primary research methods. Then systems modeling and problem definition. Then hypothesis generation and testing. Each phase has specific analytical tools, and each deliverable asks you to show your reasoning."
- [ ] **Name the Design Abilities as professional competencies.** Don't just list them. For each one, give a 10-second example of when an MBA grad would need it: "Navigate Ambiguity: you're leading a team through a reorg and nobody knows what the new structure looks like yet. Synthesize Information: you have 40 customer interviews and need to find the pattern. Experiment Rapidly: your CEO wants a recommendation by Friday and you have three possible approaches."
- [ ] **Draw the line against "startup class" early.** "This is not New Enterprises. We're not building businesses. We're building the observational and analytical muscles you need before you can build anything."

### Ongoing: Activity framing protocol

For every session, use this pattern:

1. **Frame** (2 min): Name the skill, connect to a professional context, state what you'll be looking for
2. **Do** (activity time): Let the Explore → Make Sense → Name pedagogy run as designed
3. **Debrief with vocabulary** (5 min): Name what they just did in analytical terms, connect to the Design Ability, preview how it feeds the next deliverable

This closes the loop that Section 001 students felt was missing. The activities don't change. The wrapper does.

### Assignment descriptions

- [ ] **Lead every assignment with the deliverable and scope** (what, how long, individual or team, due date) before the context and specs
- [ ] **Add a one-sentence "Why this matters" line** connecting each assignment to a professional competency: "This assignment builds your ability to [specific skill]. In practice, this is what [role] does when [situation]."
- [ ] **Simplify the token system.** Keep the concept (flexibility without penalty), but explain it in 60 seconds with one concrete example. If it still generates confusion, collapse it to: "One free extension per person, email me before the deadline."

## Classroom Management (both sections)

### Quiet signal replacement
Retire the arms-up signal. Replace with something that asks for buy-in rather than compliance:
- At the start of the next session, say: "I need a way to pull the room back together after small group work. I've been using the hands-up signal, and I can tell it's not landing well. Here's what I need from you: when I say 'let's regroup,' wrap your thought in 30 seconds and come back. Deal?"
- Alternatively, use a visible timer on screen during group work. When it hits zero, that's the signal. Students self-manage.

### Q&A discipline
When a student asks a procedural question ("what do we turn in?"), answer it directly in one sentence. Reserve the Socratic "what do you think?" approach for conceptual questions where the ambiguity serves learning. Students can tell the difference, and using Socratic method on logistics questions reads as not knowing the answer.

### Laptop policy
If you keep it, explain the reason once ("observation requires attention training, and that's harder with screens open") and don't re-litigate. Consider allowing laptops during debrief portions where students are synthesizing notes.

## What NOT to Change

Some feedback reflects the inherent discomfort of design thinking, and changing these would undermine the course:

- **The ambiguity of the project scenarios.** The vagueness is the point. But name it: "This is supposed to feel unclear. Navigating that feeling is literally Design Ability #1."
- **The Explore → Make Sense → Name sequence.** Keep it. Just close the loop harder at the end of each session.
- **Specifications-based grading.** Good pedagogy, good for your workload. Don't switch to points.
- **Observation before solutions.** Students want to jump to answers. Resist. Name why: "We slow down here so you don't solve the wrong problem. That restraint is the hardest part of design thinking, and the most valuable."
- **The printing incident.** One time, printer 7 feet from the door. Students amplified it because it fit their "disorganized" narrative. Don't apologize for it again; just don't give them another data point.
